In Arabic, letters are categorized into two main groups: Sun Letters (Shamsi) and Moon Letters (Qamari). This classification is important for pronunciation, especially when these letters follow the definite article "ال" (al).

Shamsi Letters (Sun Letters)

حُرُوفٌ شَمْسِيَّةٌ

With shamsi letters, the "ل" in "ال" is not pronounced. The first letter of the word becomes stronger (we double it when speaking).

Notice how the "ل" (lam) in the definite article "ال" is not pronounced. Instead, the first letter of the word is doubled, as indicated by the shadda (ّ) mark.

In Arabic, harakāt (حَرَكَاتٌ) are signs used to indicate vowel sounds and other pronunciation aspects. They are crucial for correct pronunciation and meaning, especially in classical texts and religious scriptures.

Short Vowels

الحَرَكَاتُ القَصِيرَةُ

Short vowels are small marks placed above or below letters to indicate short vowel sounds.

Damma (ضَمَّةٌ)

ـُ

Pronounced as "u" as in "put"

كُتُب

kutub (books)

Fatḥa (فَتْحَةٌ)

ـَ

Pronounced as "a" as in "cat"

كَتَبَ

kataba (he wrote)

Kasra (كَسْرَةٌ)

ـِ

Pronounced as "i" as in "sit"

كِتَاب

kitāb (book)

Sukun (سُكُونٌ)

ـْ

Indicates absence of a vowel (consonant with no vowel)

مِنْ

min (from)

Shadda (شَدَّةٌ)

ـّ

Indicates doubling of a consonant

رَبّ

rabb (lord)

Double Vowels/Tanween

التَّنوِينُ

Tanween refers to double vowel marks that add an "n" sound at the end of a word.

Tanween with Damma

تَنْوِينٌ بِالضَّمِّ

ـٌ

Pronounced as "un"

كِتَابٌ

kitābun (a book)

Tanween with Fatḥa

تَنْوِينٌ بِالْفَتْحِ

ـً

Pronounced as "an"

شُكْرًا

shukran (thank you)

Tanween with Kasra

تَنْوِينٌ بِالْكَسْرِ

ـٍ

Pronounced as "in"

بِسُرْعَةٍ

bisur'atin (quickly)

Long Vowels

الحَرَكَاتُ الطَّوِيلَةُ

Long vowels in Arabic are formed by combining a short vowel with one of three letters: Alif (ا), Waw (و), or Ya (ي).

Long A (Alif)

ا

Fatḥa (ـَ) + Alif (ا) = "aa"

كِتَاب

kitāb (book)

Long U (Waw)

و

Damma (ـُ) + Waw (و) = "oo"

نُور

nūr (light)

Long I (Ya)

ي

Kasra (ـِ) + Ya (ي) = "ee"

كَرِيم

karīm (generous)

Importance of Vowels in Quranic Arabic

In the Quran, vowel marks are essential as they can completely change the meaning of a word. For example:

  • عَلِمَ ('alima) means "he knew" while عُلِمَ ('ulima) means "it was known"
  • عَلَّمَ ('allama) means "he taught" - note the shadda that doubles the letter

In Arabic grammar, like many other languages, sentences are built from various components that play specific roles. Here are the key components you'll find in Arabic sentences:

The Three Main Components

Noun (اِسْمٌ)

Names people, places, things, or concepts

Verb (فِعْلٌ)

Expresses actions or states of being

Particle (حَرْفٌ)

Connects words and indicates relationships

Noun (اِسْمٌ)

اِسْمٌ

In Arabic, a noun is called "اِسْمٌ". It's a word that refers to a person, place, thing, idea, or quality.
